## §3. What we sell

We sell **engagements**, not products. The shape of an engagement is always the same: it begins with a paid working session, it scopes to one workflow, it ships a working version, and it stays operational until it doesn't need us. The engagement gets named one of four ways depending on what the client actually needs.

**AI Products & Platforms.** Internal AI systems the team uses every day — custom assistants, knowledge search, approval screens, review dashboards. Built so the team gets answers without leaving the tools they already work in, and so managers can see what was approved and why. This is where most engagements start, because it is the work people can name.

**AI Workflows & Operations.** The layer underneath — the infrastructure that moves work between tools, teams, approvals, and exceptions. Triggers, routing, context gathering, drafts, human review, audit trails. This is what makes AI useful at scale: it turns a model output into work that actually moves.

**Enterprise AI.** The same two layers above, designed for environments where IT, security, governance, and regional residency are non-negotiable. Same engineering, with the controls (SSO, role-scoped access, audit logging, EU residency, regulatory overlay) wired into the foundation rather than bolted on.

**Pilot to Production.** When a client already has an AI pilot that proved signal but hasn't survived contact with real users, data, and approvals, we review what's there, harden what needs hardening, and get it ready for wider rollout.

**Readiness Audit.** Before any of the four above, there is a paid working session — usually presented as a readiness audit. We sit with the workflow, name what is actually in the way, and decide together whether there is a real engagement underneath. It is a session, not a sales call. If there isn't a real engagement underneath, we say so.

**Pricing.** Entry projects from **€10,000**. Typical engagements **€20,000–€100,000**. Enterprise governance and operating-layer work moves above €100,000 when the scope warrants it. ## §4. Who we serve

Two kinds of organisation, intentionally.

**Mid-market companies with 50–200 employees.** A founder, a department head, or an ops lead has a workflow that is bottlenecking the business, has named the problem, has budget, and is ready to act. AI is a means, not the headline. Typical ticket: €10,000 entry project, €20,000–€60,000 build.

**Enterprises.** A division or function inside a larger organisation has an AI pilot, a governance problem, or a need to make AI usable across regions under one consistent system. The conversation involves IT, security, and legal earlier than it does in mid-market. Typical ticket: €50,000–€100,000+, with multi-phase scopes common.

Inside both, three people decide whether an engagement happens:

- **The operational owner**, who feels the problem and owns the workflow. Usually signs.
- **The IT or security evaluator**, who decides whether the system is safe to put in.
- **The curious executive**, who initiated the conversation and wants to defend the decision internally.

The site, the pitch, and this memo speak primarily to the operational owner. The IT evaluator gets reassurance in the depth pages and the compliance section. The curious executive gets the one-paragraph answer in §2.

**Good-fit signals.** A specific workflow with real volume. Data exists somewhere, even if it's messy. A named problem owner with budget. A team that wants the capability, not just the demo. Willingness to start with one workflow.

**Poor-fit signals.** "We want an AI strategy." A request for a vendor evaluation rather than a build. No named workflow. No budget yet. A buyer who wants slideware. A team that wants to use us as cheap dev capacity for a roadmap that isn't theirs.

We say no to the poor-fit ones. Not as a posture — as a discipline. Bad engagements pull the company off its centre of gravity.

## §5. Where clients start — four entry states

Every client we talk to fits one of four states. The first job of the audit, and of every conversation, is to place them correctly. The engagement is shaped from there.

**State A — "We should be doing something with AI, but we don't know what."**
The client feels the pressure (board, market, competitors) but has no AI experience. They are not stupid; they are early. The risk is they buy a strategy deck from someone else, get nothing useful, and walk away cynical. We run a paid working session, map one real workflow, and show them where AI can safely help and where it cannot. They leave with a concrete first step, not a 40-slide vision. If there is a build underneath, it is usually a **€10,000 first system**. Many clients stay in this state for one engagement, then graduate to State B for the next.

**State B — "We have a workflow problem and we think AI is part of the answer."**
The client has a specific operational bottleneck — catalogue work, customer operations, document review, approvals, exception handling — and has at least intuited that AI fits. Data exists. People are named. Budget is in the conversation. This is the most common entry. We scope to one workflow, ship a working version, and transfer it. Typical engagement: **€20,000–€60,000**. Most of our published case studies sit here.

**State C — "We have a pilot that never reached production."**
The client has a demo that proved the idea has signal. It has been parked for months because nobody knows how to harden it for real users, real data, real approvals, real exceptions, and real cost control. The system is honest, but the path to production is not. We review what's there, decide what to keep and what to replace, name the four production-readiness decisions (data, workflow logic, approvals, monitoring), and either harden the existing build or rebuild the piece that needs rebuilding. Typical engagement: **€20,000–€100,000** depending on what we have to redo.

**State D — "AI is running here, but ungoverned, fragmented, or stuck at one team."**
This is the enterprise conversation. There are pilots across divisions, no shared operating layer, no consistent governance, regional residency questions, and an AI Act overlay that needs to land before anything scales further. We design the operating layer underneath — same system organisation-wide, with regional residency and reviewers, audit trails, scoped access, and a path from "one workflow per division" to "the platform the business runs on." Typical engagement: **€50,000–€100,000+**, often multi-phase.

Every conversation starts by placing the client. Sales does not pitch into the void; sales places, then proposes. If a client doesn't fit any state, the conversation isn't ready and we say so.

## §7. How we work

The method is the same in every engagement, and it is short enough to be repeatable.

**One.** Map the workflow the team already runs — the inputs, handovers, tools, decisions, exceptions. Not the workflow as documented; the workflow as actually performed.

**Two.** Find the AI-ready step. The one place inside that map where AI can safely reduce manual effort, improve consistency, or speed a decision — without removing the person who owns it.

**Three.** Build a working version. One workflow. Real data. Real users. Small enough to ship in weeks, not quarters.

**Four.** Add human review. Approval points, confidence checks, fallbacks, audit trails — designed in from the start, not added when someone asks.

**Five.** Scale only what earns it. Expand to the next workflow when the first one is useful, reliable, and trusted enough that the team would notice if it disappeared.

There is no "Phase 0 — alignment workshop." There is the paid working session, and then there is the build. We do not do unpaid pitches. We do not do strategy decks as deliverables.

## §8. What we are not

Clarity comes faster through boundaries than through claims, so:

We are not a SaaS product. There is no platform to license. There is no seat-based pricing. The deliverable is a system built for your business and transferred to your team.

We are not a chatbot vendor. We don't sell "an AI assistant for your team" as a category. We sell the workflow it sits inside.

We are not a strategy-deck factory. We will not deliver a roadmap as a final deliverable. The audit ends with a concrete first build, not with a slide titled "Recommendations."

We are not a body shop. We don't ship engineers by the day. We ship engagements with named scope, named owners, and a named exit.

We are not a generalist consultancy that "also does AI." Operational AI is the entire practice. If a client wants strategy without build, or build without operational instinct, they should hire someone else.

## §9. Who the website is written for

The website is written for the operational owner described in §4 — the person who has a workflow, a budget, and a problem they can name. It is written for them because they sign the engagements that fund this company. It is written secondarily for the IT evaluator who will gatekeep the technical fit, and for the curious executive who is searching for a concrete first step.

The website is **not** written for a cold marketer or a cold salesperson reading without operational context. That is a deliberate filter. Writing the site for them would mean stripping out the depth that the actual buyer needs to feel confident. That trade is one we lose more often than we win.

This does not mean the site can ignore non-specialists entirely. It means the **first thirty seconds** — the hero, the capability one-liners, the closing CTA — must pass a smart-non-specialist test: a marketer or commercial lead reading the home page in two minutes should be able to answer "what does AIHLPR do, for whom, and what is the first thing I'd buy from them." Everything past those thirty seconds is allowed to be operational. The home page hero, the section eyebrows, and the four capability headlines are the only places the language has to flex toward the non-specialist. The rest holds.

When feedback like "I don't get what you do" arrives from a non-buyer reviewer, the response is not panic. It is, in order: (1) confirm whether they made it through the first thirty seconds; (2) if they did and still didn't get it, look at this memo's §2 and ask whether the hero is failing the test; (3) only then change the page — and bring the change back into this memo so it stays the source of truth.
